The Strength of the Circle

Why I Am Requires We Are

We live in a culture obsessed with the Self. We celebrate the self-made billionaire, the solo adventurer, and the individual spark of genius. We have been conditioned to believe that our success is a ladder we climb alone. But there is a profound African philosophy that suggests we have been looking at the world through a cracked lens.

"I am because we are."

Ubuntu

The Illusion of the Individual

In the West, we often follow the Cartesian logic of "I think, therefore I am." It places the center of the universe squarely between our own ears. It suggests that our identity is a private project. But if you take a human being and strip away every relationship, every teacher who coached them, every parent who comforted them, and every colleague who challenged them, what is left?

The truth is that none of us exists in a vacuum. A leader is not a leader without a team that chooses to follow. A speaker is not a speaker without an audience that chooses to listen. Our very humanity is a quality that is conferred upon us by others. Ubuntu reminds us that we do not find ourselves by looking inward. We find ourselves by looking at the people around us.

The Ecosystem of Humanity

Imagine a forest. We might point to a single, towering oak and marvel at its strength. But beneath the soil, that tree is part of a complex, invisible network of roots and fungi that share nutrients and warnings. If the forest dies, the tree dies, no matter how strong it thinks it is.

We are all one. We breathe the same air, we feel the same pain, and we live under the same sky. There is no such thing as a truly separate life. When we hurt someone else, we are essentially wounding the collective body that we all belong to. When we lift someone up, we are strengthening the air we all breathe.

When we practice Ubuntu, we realize that:

Your success is my success.
If the team wins, the individual thrives.
Your suffering is my suffering.
If a member of the community is marginalized, the integrity of the whole community is compromised.
Kindness is a survival strategy.
Treating others with dignity is not just nice. It is the only way to sustain the environment that allows us to exist.

Leading with Ubuntu

In the world of business and leadership, we often prioritize What and How. We focus on the bottom line and the efficiency of the process. But Ubuntu asks us to focus on the Who. A company operating with the spirit of Ubuntu does not just see human resources. It sees a community of people whose lives are inextricably linked.

When a leader says, "I am because we are," they are acknowledging that their authority is a gift from the people they serve. They realize that their job is not to be the smartest person in the room, but to create a room where everyone feels safe enough to be their best selves.

The Way Forward

We do not need more self-help. We need us-help. The challenges we face today — from the loneliness epidemic to global instability — cannot be solved by individuals acting in isolation. They require us to rebuild our circles. They require us to acknowledge that my humanity is caught up, bound up, inextricably, in yours.

When we shift our perspective from Me to We, something incredible happens. The pressure to be perfect evaporates, replaced by the support of the collective. We stop competing for a bigger slice of the pie and start focusing on making a bigger pie for everyone.

"I am because we are."
It is not just a philosophy.
It is a biological and social reality.
And the sooner we embrace it, the sooner we can start
building a world where everyone truly belongs.
